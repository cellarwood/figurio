# Campaign Planning

Use this skill when turning a Figurio product moment into a marketing plan that can actually ship.

Figurio campaigns should create demand for premium, giftable, highly visual figurines without overstating production, timing, or customization. The goal is not generic awareness. The goal is launch momentum, repeatable seasonal demand, and content that people want to share.

## What This Skill Owns

- Launch campaign structure.
- Seasonal drop planning.
- UGC and creator campaign logic.
- Email, social, landing page, and retargeting message alignment.
- Trust-building campaign elements such as proof, timing, and fulfillment clarity.
- Performance framing for leadership.

## Use It For

- New product launches.
- Holiday, gifting, and seasonal drops.
- Creator collaborations and UGC prompts.
- Waitlist, preorder, or early-access campaigns.
- Post-purchase referral and sharing loops.
- Re-engagement for past buyers or warm prospects.

## Planning Principles

1. Start with the customer reason to care, not the asset list.
2. Build around one sharp promise per campaign.
3. Tie the campaign to a moment, occasion, or product milestone whenever possible.
4. Prefer a small number of strong concepts over many weak variants.
5. Reuse one narrative spine across channels.
6. Match the campaign promise to actual production and fulfillment reality.
7. Optimize for shareability without sacrificing premium perception.

## Figurio Campaign Anchors

These are the campaign angles that usually fit the brand:

- Giftability: a figurine that feels personal and worth giving.
- Visual delight: the product should be easy to understand from imagery alone.
- Identity and memory: people buy to capture a person, pet, moment, or fandom-like affinity.
- Seasonal relevance: holidays, graduations, birthdays, weddings, events, and end-of-year gifting.
- Social proof: real photos, reactions, unboxings, testimonials, and creator content.
- Trust: clear production windows, review steps, materials, and support expectations.

## Planning Workflow

1. Define the campaign objective in one sentence.
2. Name the primary audience.
3. Pick one primary message.
4. Choose one desired action.
5. Identify one proof point that makes the message believable.
6. Decide the campaign window and trigger.
7. Map the message into site, social, email, and paid assets.
8. List production or fulfillment dependencies before writing copy.
9. Define what success looks like for both demand and trust.
10. Decide what not to do so the campaign stays focused.

## Campaign Brief Minimums

Every Figurio campaign brief should include:

- Objective.
- Audience.
- Primary message.
- Supporting proof.
- Offer or call to action.
- Launch date or window.
- Channel mix.
- Asset list.
- Dependencies and risks.
- Success metrics.

## Channel Logic

### Website and landing pages

- Lead with the campaign idea and product truth.
- Put proof, timing, and CTA near the top.
- Keep the page consistent with the campaign narrative used elsewhere.

### Email

- Use email to move warm audiences through the campaign sequence.
- Keep subject lines specific, premium, and concrete.
- Align each send to one purpose: announce, remind, reassure, or convert.

### Organic social

- Use social to create recognition, urgency, and shareable proof.
- Favor formats that show the product immediately: reels, carousels, UGC, before/after, reveal, and unboxing.
- Repost customer content when it reinforces trust and desire.

### Paid social

- Use paid only when the creative can stand on its own.
- Keep the hook simple and the visual unmistakable.
- Retarget with proof, not just repetition.

### Creator and UGC

- Give creators a concrete angle, not a vague brand request.
- Ask for specific situations, reactions, or use cases.
- Keep disclosures and product claims accurate.

## Message Structure

Use this order unless there is a strong reason not to:

1. What the product is.
2. Why it matters now.
3. Why this version is worth attention.
4. Why it is credible.
5. What the audience should do next.

## Trust Rules

- Never imply faster production, deeper customization, or stronger guarantees than operations can support.
- Never hide delivery windows or approval steps when the campaign depends on them.
- Never use urgency language that contradicts inventory, lead time, or review reality.
- If the campaign depends on a promise, verify that support and operations can sustain it.

## Seasonal Planning Rules

- Start early enough to create content, proof, and review cycles before the peak window.
- Build a single seasonal story that can stretch across multiple surfaces.
- Reuse the same campaign spine in social, email, and landing page copy.
- Add specific gift or occasion framing only when it helps the audience decide.

## UGC Rules

- Ask for recognizable, easy-to-shot moments.
- Prompt reactions, reveals, comparisons, gifting moments, and unboxing.
- Reward authenticity over polish when the content is for social proof.
- Make it easy for customers to participate without a production burden.

## Measurement

Track the campaign with a small set of metrics that match the objective:

- Reach or impressions when the goal is visibility.
- CTR and landing page engagement when the goal is interest.
- Email opens and clicks when the goal is sequence performance.
- Conversion rate, preorders, or sales when the goal is revenue.
- UGC volume, saves, shares, and reposts when the goal is social proof.
- Support load or complaint rate when the campaign introduces new promises.

## Output Format

When you produce a campaign plan, keep it compact and usable:

- Campaign name
- Objective
- Audience
- Core message
- Proof point
- Channels
- Key assets
- Timeline
- Risks
- Success metrics
- Next actions

## Review Checklist

- Is there one clear reason to care?
- Is the campaign anchored in a real moment or product milestone?
- Can the same message drive site, social, email, and paid?
- Are proof and trust visible early enough?
- Are the promises operationally safe?
- Is the campaign specific enough to feel premium and memorable?
