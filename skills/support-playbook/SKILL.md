# Figurio Support Playbook

Use this skill when responding to customer support requests, triaging cases, drafting customer-facing replies, or preparing a handoff for operations or escalation.

## Core Goal

Protect customer trust while moving every case toward a concrete next step. Be accurate before being fast.

## Operating Principles

- Start from the order record, not from the customer's interpretation of events.
- Separate facts, assumptions, and promises.
- Be calm, concise, and transparent when the answer is incomplete.
- Never promise a refund, replacement, shipping outcome, or approval exception before it is confirmed by the responsible owner.
- Prefer one clear next action over a long list of possibilities.
- Preserve the customer's confidence by acknowledging the issue without overexplaining internal uncertainty.

## First Response Checklist

- Confirm the order ID, customer name, and issue type.
- Check the latest production, approval, fulfillment, and shipping status.
- Identify whether the case is informational, operational, financial, or trust-sensitive.
- Decide whether support can resolve it directly or needs escalation.
- Reply with what is known, what is still being checked, and when the customer should expect the next update.

## Common Case Handling

### Shipping Delays

- Verify whether the order is still in production, handed to the carrier, or in an exception state.
- Distinguish internal production delay from carrier delay.
- Share the last confirmed scan or handoff point when available.
- If tracking is stale, say so plainly and note the next checkpoint.
- Do not blame the carrier or factory without evidence.

### Damaged Items

- Ask for photos of the item, packaging, shipping label, and visible damage.
- Capture the order ID, delivery date, and brief description of the damage.
- Do not debate whether the item "looks bad enough" in the first reply.
- Treat damage cases as evidence-driven and hand them off cleanly if replacement or refund approval is needed.

### Custom-Approval Disputes

- Explain the approval status and the exact reason the order is waiting.
- If the customer believes they already approved or were not shown a revision, restate the current record without arguing.
- Keep the language neutral: confirm the approval history, then route any discrepancy for review.
- Never imply that production will continue until the approval gate is actually cleared.

### Refund Requests

- Confirm the reason for the request and whether the issue is shipping, damage, approval, or dissatisfaction.
- Check whether a refund is eligible under the current order state and policy.
- If the request needs review, tell the customer that it has been passed to the responsible owner.
- Avoid using the word "approved" unless approval has already been granted.

## Response Standards

- Use short paragraphs and one request at a time when evidence is needed.
- Repeat the essential facts back to the customer so they can confirm them.
- Use exact language for uncertainty: "I have not confirmed that yet" is better than vague reassurance.
- If the case is delayed internally, tell the customer what is being checked and what will happen next.
- End with a concrete expectation: next update, evidence requested, owner reviewing, or status already confirmed.

## Handoff Bundle

When escalating or handing off, include:

- Order ID and customer contact.
- Issue category and one-sentence summary.
- Relevant dates, scans, approval events, or delivery milestones.
- Customer message history if the wording matters.
- Evidence received, especially photos or screenshots.
- The exact decision needed from operations, finance, or fulfillment.

## Workspace Use

- Use Gmail for intake, threaded replies, and confirmation of sensitive requests.
- Use Docs for customer reply drafts, incident notes, and reusable approved language.
- Use Sheets when a shared case log is needed for delayed, refunded, or damage-related orders.
- Use Calendar only when the case requires a scheduled follow-up or timed operational checkpoint.
